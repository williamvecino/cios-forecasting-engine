Use this 6-panel structure.

It is the cleanest way to make CIOS feel like a real decision-intelligence product without touching the engine.

The 6-panel enterprise decision layout
Panel 1 — Question Header

Top of page. Large and clean.

Purpose:
anchor the entire screen to one decision question.

Contents:

strategic question

asset / market / therapy area tags

time horizon

status

last updated timestamp

button for new scenario

button for view ledger

Example:

Will ARIKAYCE achieve target specialist adoption (≥4 Rx/quarter) within 12 months?

Rare disease / Orphan | Adoption | 12-month horizon | High confidence
Last updated: Mar 17, 2026

This is what makes the interface feel question-first rather than dashboard-first.

Panel 2 — Primary Forecast Card

This is the centerpiece.

Purpose:
show the current answer immediately.

Contents:

current probability

prior probability

change from prior

confidence label

optional forecast band

one-line interpretation

Example:

Current probability
72.2%

Prior: 61.0%
Change: +11.2 pts
Confidence: High

Interpretation:
Current signals support above-threshold specialist adoption within the forecast window.

This should be visually dominant.

Panel 3 — Key Drivers

Right below or beside the forecast card.

Purpose:
explain why the probability is what it is.

Contents:

top 3 to 5 positive drivers

top 2 to 4 negative drivers

simple impact labels: high / medium / low

optional source type tags: clinical, payer, field, competitor, guideline

Example:

Top Drivers

Positive
+ Expanded payer coverage (High)
+ Positive real-world evidence trend (Medium)
+ Strong specialist concentration in target accounts (Medium)

Negative
− Workflow friction in community settings (Medium)
− Competing narrative around watchful waiting (Low)

This is one of the most important panels. It turns the system from a number machine into an interpretable engine.

Panel 4 — Signal Stack

This is the operational evidence layer.

Purpose:
show the validated signals feeding the current forecast.

Contents:

signal ID

signal description

direction

strength

reliability

validation status

timestamp

linked statement or category

Example table:

SIG-021 | Positive RWE expansion in target subgroup | Positive | Strength 4 | Reliability 4 | Validated
SIG-019 | Payer coverage widened in priority accounts | Positive | Strength 5 | Reliability 5 | Validated
SIG-017 | Competitor launch discussion increasing | Negative | Strength 3 | Reliability 3 | Validated

Design note:
Do not show raw math here. Show structured signals only.

Panel 5 — Scenario Simulator

This is the panel that makes it feel Aaru-like.

Purpose:
allow strategic exploration without changing the live forecast.

Contents:

selectable hypothetical signals

scenario presets

recompute button

base probability vs scenario probability

delta

applied scenario assumptions

label clearly as scenario

Example:

Scenario: Competitor trial succeeds

Base probability: 72.2%
Scenario probability: 49.5%
Delta: −22.7 pts

Applied assumptions:
- Positive competitor efficacy readout
- Increased KOL attention to competitor
- Delayed guideline influence for ARIKAYCE

Best UX pattern:
use cards or toggles, not giant forms.

Three preset scenario buttons work well:

Best case

Base case

Risk case

Then allow custom signal toggles below.

This panel is what moves CIOS from dashboard to strategic planning platform.

Panel 6 — Recommended Action

Bottom-right or full-width summary block.

Purpose:
translate forecast into decision guidance.

Contents:

short recommendation headline

rationale

risk note

next signals to watch

Example:

Recommended Action

Accelerate specialist-focused field deployment over the next 90 days.

Rationale:
The forecast is currently supported by favorable payer and evidence signals, but this advantage could compress quickly if competitive clinical momentum increases.

Monitor next:
- competitor trial readout
- payer restrictions in top 20 accounts
- new guideline commentary

This is critical. Executives do not want only a probability. They want “what now?”

That is the core layout.

Why this works

This layout mirrors how senior decision-makers think:

What is the question?

What is the answer?

Why do we think that?

What evidence supports it?

What happens if conditions change?

What should we do now?

That sequence is exactly why these systems feel expensive and useful.

Build the next CIOS layer as a deterministic adopter/archetype engine that connects the structural adopter segments already produced by the system to behavioral decision profiles and reaction simulation.

Core principle

Do not replace the current adoption segments.

Keep the existing segment outputs such as:

Early Adopters
Persuadables
Late Movers
Resistant

Keep the current structural descriptors such as:

specialty
center type
center location / region
infrastructure readiness

These structural segments describe who they are and whether they can adopt.

Add a new behavioral layer that describes how they decide.

The rule is:

Segment = structural readiness
Archetype = decision behavior

They intersect. They do not replace each other.

What to build

Add a new layer called:

Adopter Archetype Engine

This layer should sit:

after segmentation
before reaction simulation

Workflow becomes:

Signals
→ Gates
→ Adoption Segments
→ Archetype Assignment
→ Reaction Simulation
→ Decide / Respond

Objective

For every adopter segment the system generates, assign one or more likely behavioral archetypes based on observable characteristics, then use those archetypes to estimate the most likely reactions to evidence, visuals, slides, access changes, guideline updates, and other market events.

This must be deterministic and rule-based, not freeform persona generation.

Archetypes to implement first

Start with these five canonical archetypes.

1. Evidence-Driven Innovator

Primary driver: clinical evidence quality
Typical behavior: adopts early when efficacy or survival signal is strong
Risk tolerance: high
Guideline dependence: low
Operational sensitivity: low to moderate
Typical environments: academic centers, trial-experienced specialists, KOLs

2. Operational Pragmatist

Primary driver: workflow feasibility and implementation burden
Typical behavior: interested in innovation but delays adoption if staffing, logistics, or infrastructure are weak
Risk tolerance: moderate
Guideline dependence: low to moderate
Operational sensitivity: high
Typical environments: community settings, capacity-constrained centers, practices with limited support staff

3. Guideline Follower

Primary driver: formal consensus, institutional protocol, guideline endorsement
Typical behavior: waits for NCCN / society / institutional inclusion before meaningful use
Risk tolerance: low
Guideline dependence: high
Operational sensitivity: moderate
Typical environments: hospital systems, protocol-driven institutions, committee-dependent settings

4. Financial Gatekeeper

Primary driver: reimbursement clarity, payer behavior, cost exposure
Typical behavior: delays adoption until coverage is stable and access friction is low
Risk tolerance: low
Guideline dependence: moderate
Operational sensitivity: moderate
Typical environments: practices with restrictive payer mix, access-sensitive systems, pharmacy or admin-led settings

5. Skeptical Conservative

Primary driver: long-term safety, real-world evidence, proven durability
Typical behavior: resists adoption until post-launch evidence accumulates
Risk tolerance: very low
Guideline dependence: moderate to high
Operational sensitivity: moderate
Typical environments: conservative clinicians, established treatment users, late-switch institutions

Design principle

Segments describe capacity and adoption position.
Archetypes describe decision style and reaction pattern.

Both are required for realistic adoption forecasting.

The system should move from:

who adopts first
to
why they adopt
to
how they react to evidence, access, and operational changes

That is the purpose of this build.

Final instruction

Build this as a bounded, deterministic behavioral layer on top of the existing segment outputs. Use stable healthcare adoption archetypes, observable structural characteristics, and explicit response rules. Do not turn this into a loose persona generator. The goal is to make CIOS predict how identified adopter groups are most likely to react under real market constraints.
